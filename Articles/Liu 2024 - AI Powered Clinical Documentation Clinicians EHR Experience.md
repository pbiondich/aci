## Article Title
AI-Powered Clinical Documentation and Clinicians' Electronic Health Record Experience: A Nonrandomized Clinical Trial

## Author(s)
Liu, Tsai-Ling; Hetherington, Timothy C.; Stephens, Cassidy; McWilliams, Andrew; Dharod, Ajay; Carroll, Tracey; Cleveland, Jeffrey A.

## DOI
[10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.32460](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.32460)

## Journal / Source
JAMA Network Open | Research Letter | September 6, 2024. Open Access (CC-BY). Atrium Health / Wake Forest University School of Medicine, Charlotte/Winston-Salem NC. TREND reporting guideline. IRB exempted.

## Ambient Product(s) Evaluated
**DAX Copilot** (Nuance/Microsoft). Dragon Ambient eXperience. Atrium Health outpatient clinics in North Carolina and Georgia. Same institution as Bundy 2024 (Atrium Health) — this is the quantitative companion to Bundy's qualitative study.

## Clinical Setting
Atrium Health — all outpatient clinics in North Carolina and Georgia. Nonrandomized clinical trial. **Intervention group** (n=112): family medicine, internal medicine, general pediatrics clinicians who accepted invitation; enrolled in 4 waves June–August 2023. **Control group** (n=117): clinicians who did not use DAX — assembled via service line leaders + clinicians who initially expressed interest then opted out. Power: 83 intervention + 55 control for 80% power to detect differences. Survey administered before and 5 weeks after intervention. Anonymous 7-question survey (AMA Organizational Biopsy EHR questions, 1–5 scale). n=140 respondents (85 intervention, 55 control).

## Evidence Level
Observational (nonrandomized clinical trial — intervention vs. control group, pre/post survey)

## Data Source(s)
Survey (7-item pre/post; AMA Organizational Biopsy EHR instrument) | χ² comparison between groups

## Article Summary
This nonrandomized trial at Atrium Health compared DAX Copilot users (n=85 survey respondents) vs. non-users (n=55 controls) before and 5 weeks after DAX deployment. Key findings using χ² tests comparing proportions reporting improvement: Intervention group had significantly higher rates of reporting (1) decreased EHR time at home (47.1% vs. 14.5%, p<.001), (2) decreased weekly EHR time outside work hours (44.7% vs. 20.0%, p=.003), (3) decreased post-visit documentation time (43.5% vs. 18.2%, p=.002), and (4) less EHR frustration (44.7% vs. 14.5%, p<.001). However, ~55% of intervention group and ~69% of controls reported "unchanged" experience — the majority in both groups saw no change. Authors note approximately half of clinicians using DAX reported positive outcomes; a significant subset did not. Limitations: selection bias (opt-in intervention), recall bias, 5-week follow-up is short.

## Hypothesis
To evaluate clinicians' experiences with clinical documentation before and after implementing an AI-powered clinical documentation tool, compared to a control group not using the tool.

*📍 Companion paper to Bundy 2024 (JGIM qualitative) — same Atrium Health institution, same DAX deployment, complementary methods. Bundy captured the "why" qualitatively; Liu captures the "how many" quantitatively.*
*📍 Research Letter format — concise 3-page paper. Only 7 survey items, χ² test only. No EHR telemetry. Short follow-up (5 weeks). Findings are suggestive rather than definitive.*
*📍 The "majority saw no change" finding (55% in intervention) is important context for the positive results: the ~45% who reported improvement drove the significant comparisons.*
*📍 Control group construction is problematic: includes both non-selected clinicians AND clinicians who opted out of DAX after informational meetings. Opt-outs likely differ from opt-ins in systematic ways (technology adoption barriers, workflow preference, specialty).*
*📍 Notable: Significant EHR after-hours improvement — consistent with Duggan 2025 finding. Two papers from the same general Atrium Health / Wake Forest ecosystem (Liu 2024 and Bundy 2024) agree that DAX reduces after-hours burden at this institution.*
