# Article Review

---

## Article Title
Impact of using an AI scribe on clinical documentation and clinician-patient interactions in allied health private practice: perspectives of clinicians and patients

## Author(s)
Evans, Kerrie; Papinniemi, Amy; Ploderer, Bernd; Nicholson, Vaughan; Hindhaugh, Tom; Vuvan, Viana; Cowley, Nicholas; Tariq, Amina; Thomson, Hayley

## DOI
[10.1016/j.msksp.2025.103333](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.msksp.2025.103333)

## Journal / Source
Musculoskeletal Science and Practice | Vol. 78 | August 2025 | Article 103333. Open Access (CC BY 4.0). University of Sydney / University of Queensland / Queensland University of Technology / Australian Catholic University / Healthia Limited.

## Ambient Product(s) Evaluated
**Lyrebird Health** (Lyrebird) — AI scribe product. Transcribes AHP-patient conversations, then generates consultation notes, referral letters, and other clinical documentation. AHPs review and edit before transferring to practice management software. Not integrated directly with the EHR. Product had been piloted by 8 clinicians in the 6 months prior to the study.

## Clinical Setting
Australian allied health private practice within **Healthia Limited** — a privately owned allied healthcare company operating on a partnership model. 876 full-time AHPs in the organization; 119 participated. Physiotherapists (76%), podiatrists (10%), occupational therapists (8%), exercise physiologists (5%). 75% metropolitan, 23% regional, 2% rural. 46% female. Study period: February–July 2024. Mixed-methods: AHP surveys at baseline, 6 weeks, 3 months; patient survey once after AI-scribed appointment; semi-structured interviews with 15 AHPs + 14 patients after 3 months.

## Evidence Level
Observational (longitudinal mixed-methods QI study — quantitative surveys + semi-structured interviews)

## Data Source(s)
AHP surveys (baseline, 6 weeks, 3 months) | Patient surveys (cross-sectional, one time) | Semi-structured interviews (15 AHPs, 14 patients) | Productivity data from practice management software

## Article Summary
This is the only study in the corpus examining an AI scribe in **allied health** settings (physiotherapy, podiatry, OT, exercise physiology) and the only Australian private practice study. 119 AHPs and 157 patients participated across a large private practice network over 3 months. Quantitative results: significant reduction in time spent on notes, letters, and out-of-hours documentation between baseline and 6 weeks, and baseline and 3 months — but no further change between 6 and 3 months, suggesting benefits plateau quickly. Documentation burden significantly less at 3 months vs. baseline (Cohen's d = 0.22). Productivity increased by an average of **5.8%**. 95% of AHPs at 3 months agreed or strongly agreed they would recommend continued AI scribe use. Job satisfaction was mixed — only 29% agreed AI improved job satisfaction (23% somewhat agreed). Patient perspective: comfortable consenting to AI scribe; some noted other patients might need more information about data storage. Qualitative themes: positive impact on therapeutic alliance and administrative workload; trust facilitated uptake; AHPs valued scribe for different aspects of documentation. Notable: patient opt-outs during 3-month period totaled only 74 out of an estimated 64,099 appointments — extremely low refusal rate.

## Hypothesis
Explicit: to describe perspectives and experiences of AHPs and patients regarding the use of an AI scribe for clinical documentation in Australian allied health private practice.

*📍 Only allied health study in the entire corpus. Physiotherapists, podiatrists, OTs, exercise physiologists — not physicians. Extends ambient AI research beyond medicine into AHP settings.*
*📍 Only Australian study in the corpus (alongside Foo 2025 if obtained). Private practice fee-for-service setting — different economics and incentives from US academic health systems.*
*📍 Productivity increase of 5.8% is an objective, operationally meaningful measure (from practice management software). This is one of the few papers in the corpus providing a non-EHR productivity measure.*
*📍 Benefits plateau by 6 weeks — no further improvement 6wks→3months. This suggests rapid adoption of available gains, not continued improvement. Implementation planners should set expectations accordingly.*
*📍 Job satisfaction mixed (29% vs. 95% recommendation): AHPs recommend the tool but don't necessarily feel their job satisfaction improved — important distinction. Administrative relief ≠ job satisfaction.*
*📍 Patient opt-out rate ~0.1% (74/64,099 estimated appointments) is reassuring for consent/acceptability, though the denominator is an estimate.*
