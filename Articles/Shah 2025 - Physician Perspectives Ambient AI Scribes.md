## Article Title
Physician Perspectives on Ambient AI Scribes

## Author(s)
Shah, Shreya J.; Crowell, Trevor; Jeong, Yejin; Devon-Sand, Anna; Smith, Margaret; Yang, Betsy; Ma, Stephen P.; Liang, April S.; Delahaie, Clarissa; Hsia, Caroline; Shanafelt, Tait; Pfeffer, Michael A.; Sharp, Christopher; Lin, Steven; Garcia, Patricia

## DOI
[10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2025.1904](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2025.1904)

## Journal / Source
JAMA Network Open | 2025

## Ambient Product(s) Evaluated
**Nuance DAX Copilot** — deployed at Stanford Medicine (Stanford University School of Medicine / Stanford Healthcare). The qualitative study focuses on provider perspectives from a real-world DAX Copilot pilot.

## Clinical Setting
Stanford Medicine — ambulatory outpatient settings across community and faculty practice. 22 physician interviews: primary care (community 8, faculty 5) and ambulatory specialty (community 4, faculty 5). Stratified by utilization level (high/medium).

## Evidence Level
Qualitative (semi-structured interviews; thematic analysis guided by RE-AIM PRISM framework)

## Data Source(s)
Survey/self-report (qualitative interview transcripts; codebook guided by RE-AIM PRISM framework)

## Article Summary
This qualitative study at Stanford conducted 22 semi-structured physician interviews to understand perspectives on ambient AI scribes (DAX Copilot). The analysis was structured by the RE-AIM PRISM implementation science framework. Physicians were stratified by utilization level and practice setting. Themes centered on workload relief, patient engagement, documentation accuracy concerns, note quality variability, and implementation barriers. The study is a companion to Ma et al. (2025) — which covers the quantitative utilization data from the same Stanford DAX pilot — and together they form a comprehensive mixed-methods picture from Stanford.

## Hypothesis
Not explicitly stated. Exploratory qualitative study using implementation science framework (RE-AIM PRISM) to identify facilitators and barriers.

## Study Measures

**D&M Dimensions reference:**
- **SQ** = System Quality | **IQ** = Information Quality | **SerQ** = Service Quality
- **U** = Use / Intention to Use | **US** = User Satisfaction | **NB** = Net Benefits

| Measure | Description | D&M Dimension(s) |
|---|---|---|
| Workload Relief (qualitative) | Physician-reported experience of reduced administrative burden from using ambient AI, including time savings and cognitive offloading during clinical care. | [[US - User Satisfaction]], [[II - Individual Impact]] |
| Patient Engagement | Perceived improvement in physician presence and quality of interaction with patients when freed from note-taking during encounters. | [[US - User Satisfaction]], [[OI - Organizational Impact]] |
| Documentation Accuracy Concerns | Physician-reported concerns about note accuracy, hallucinations, and the burden of reviewing/editing AI-generated content before sign-off. | [[IQ - Information Quality]], [[US - User Satisfaction]] |
| Note Quality Variability | Provider perceptions of inconsistency in AI note quality across encounter types, specialties, and patient complexity levels. | [[IQ - Information Quality]] |
| Implementation Barriers | Qualitative coding of structural, technical, and workflow barriers to sustained adoption — including EHR integration friction, training gaps, and access issues. | [[SerQ - Service Quality]], [[U - Use]] |
| Utilization Stratification | Physicians grouped by self-reported usage level (high vs. medium). Used to explore whether adoption intensity correlates with attitude and outcome patterns. | [[U - Use]] |
