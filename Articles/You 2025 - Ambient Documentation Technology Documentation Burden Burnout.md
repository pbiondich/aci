## Article Title
Ambient Documentation Technology in Clinician Experience of Documentation Burden and Burnout

## Author(s)
You, Jacqueline G.; Dbouk, Reema H.; Landman, Adam; Ting, David Y.; Dutta, Sayon; Wang, Julie C.; Centi, Amanda J.; Macfarlane, Molly; Bechor, Eran; Letourneau, Jonathan; Choo-Kang, Gabrielle; Kim, Esther H.; Magee, Cordula; Lang, Brian J.; Angelo, Laura; Olin, Jackson; Frits, Michelle; Iannaccone, Christine; Rui, Angela; Salmikova, Ivana; Holland, Christopher; Blanchette, Bryan; Silverman, Rachel; Bates, David W.; Rotenstein, Lisa; Mishuris, Rebecca G.

JAMA Network Open | 2025

## Ambient Product(s) Evaluated
Not specified by name. Ambient documentation technology deployed across Mass General Brigham (MGB, Boston) and Emory Healthcare (Atlanta). Mixed-methods evaluation of a real-world implementation.

## Clinical Setting
Two large academic health systems: Mass General Brigham (Boston) and Emory Healthcare (Atlanta). Multi-specialty outpatient and inpatient settings. Mixed-methods pre/post survey design.

## Evidence Level
Observational (pre/post mixed-methods: quantitative surveys + qualitative analysis)

## Data Source(s)
Survey/self-report | Mixed (qualitative + quantitative survey instruments)

## Article Summary
This mixed-methods study evaluated ambient documentation technology at MGB and Emory, using pre- and post-implementation surveys to assess clinician experience with documentation burden and burnout. The study collected both quantitative Likert-scale responses and qualitative free-text data across multiple specialty types. Survey domains included EHR burden, burnout indicators, well-being, and documentation workflow experience. The study provides one of the larger multi-site mixed-methods evaluations in the corpus and is notable for including both academic and community settings across two geographically distinct health systems.

## Hypothesis
Not explicitly stated. Implicit hypothesis: implementation of ambient documentation technology will reduce self-reported documentation burden and improve measures of burnout and clinician well-being.
