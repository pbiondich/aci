## Article Title
Clinician Experiences With Ambient Scribe Technology to Assist With Documentation Burden and Efficiency

## Author(s)
Hanson, William; Howell, John T. III; Sheinberg, Michael; Johnson, Kevin B.; Duggan, Maeve (listed in CSV as lead author); et al.

## DOI
[10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.60637](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2024.60637)

## Journal / Source
JAMA Network Open | 2025. Open Access. University of Pennsylvania Health System / Thomas Jefferson University / Lancaster General Health / CHOP. Accompanied by invited editorial commentary: "Ambient Listening — Legal and Ethical Issues" (Cohen, Ritzman, Cahill — also cited in Guo 2026).

## Ambient Product(s) Evaluated
**DAX Copilot** (Nuance/Microsoft). University of Pennsylvania Health System ambulatory practices.

## Clinical Setting
University of Pennsylvania Health System. 46 ambulatory clinicians (40 physicians, 2 NPs, 4 PAs) across 16 specialties. Mean 11.1 years in practice, mean 36.5 visits/week. Mixed-methods study: pre/post EHR telemetry + pre/post survey + qualitative open-ended comments. Focused on EHR efficiency metrics and clinician perception. No concurrent control group.

## Evidence Level
Observational (pre/post mixed-methods — EHR telemetry + survey + qualitative)

## Data Source(s)
EHR telemetry (Epic Signal — 5 metrics) | Survey (pre/post — 16 targeted perception questions, 0–10 scale) | Qualitative (open-ended comments, Table 4 themes)

## Article Summary
This mixed-methods study at Penn Medicine evaluated DAX Copilot across 46 ambulatory clinicians, using both EHR telemetry and detailed survey instruments. EHR Signal findings: time in notes per appointment −15.2% (10.3→8.2 min, p<.001); same-day encounter closure **increased** +9.1% (66.2%→72.4%, p<.001); **after-hours ("pajama time") decreased −16.9%** (50.6→35.4 min, p=.02) — notably, this is one of the few papers with a significant after-hours objective reduction; portion of notes manually typed decreased −20.7% (11.2%→7.9%, p<.001). Note length increased +15.5% in total characters per week (p<.001). Survey: the most dramatic finding was in documentation-during-visit cognitive burden items — all four burden questions showed ~3.4-point drop on 0–10 scale (p<.001), reflecting the largest cognitive relief signal in the corpus. Feeling able to see more patients did NOT change significantly (p=.93) — consistent with Bundy 2024's concern that this is a feared but not realized pressure. Qualitative themes (Table 4): efficiency/time savings, patient engagement, documentation burden, note quality concerns, future usability. Rich verbatim clinician quotes — including explicitly negative ones.

## Hypothesis
To evaluate clinicians' experiences with clinical documentation before and after implementing an AI-powered ambient scribing tool (DAX Copilot).

*📍 Notable: After-hours time significantly reduced (−16.9%, p=.02) — one of the few papers in the corpus to show this objectively. This may be because Penn Medicine had higher baseline pajama time (50.6 min/day) than other sites, giving more room for improvement.*
*📍 The 16-item targeted perception survey with all ~3.4-point drops on cognitive burden items is among the most striking clinician experience signals in the corpus.*
*📍 "Feel able to see more patients" NOT significant (p=.93) — reinforces Bundy 2024 finding. Clinicians don't perceive increased capacity even when efficiency improves.*
*📍 Invited commentary "Ambient Listening — Legal and Ethical Issues" (Cohen, Ritzman, Cahill) published simultaneously — signals growing attention to legal/regulatory concerns. Referenced also in Guo 2026.*
