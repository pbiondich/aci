## Article Title
Implementing Digital Scribes to Reduce Electronic Health Record Documentation Burden Among Cancer Care Clinicians: A Mixed-Methods Pilot Study

## Author(s)
Nguyen, Oliver T.; Turner, Kea; Charles, Dannelle; Sprowls, Christopher; et al.

## DOI
[10.1200/CCI.22.00166](https://doi.org/10.1200/CCI.22.00166)

## Journal / Source
JCO Clinical Cancer Informatics | 2023. Mixed-methods longitudinal pilot study at Moffitt Cancer Center (NCI-designated Comprehensive Cancer Center). Open Access.

## Ambient Product(s) Evaluated
**Dragon Ambient eXperience (DAX)** versions 3.0.5, 3.0.6, and 3.0.7 (Nuance: Burlington, MA). Third-party DS smartphone app. Recordings not stored on smartphone — securely transmitted to vendor's servers for processing into visit notes. Vendor staff completed preliminary editing before releasing to clinician.

## Clinical Setting
Moffitt Cancer Center (NCI-designated Comprehensive Cancer Center in southeastern US). 21 clinician champions participated in pilot. 10 completed baseline survey (47.6% response); 9 completed 1-month survey; 8 completed interview. Cancer care clinicians (physicians and APPs).

## Evidence Level
Mixed-methods longitudinal pilot study (pre/post surveys + semistructured interviews)

## Data Source(s)
Surveys (demographics, Mini-Z burnout, sleep quality, implementation outcomes) | Semistructured interviews (CFIR framework) | Complete case analysis for missing data

## Article Summary
This mixed-methods pilot at Moffitt Cancer Center assessed a digital scribe (DS) among cancer care clinicians. DS rated as marginally acceptable (16.0), appropriate (16.3), and usable (68.6). Feasibility slightly below cutoff (15.2 vs 16.0). No significant improvement in burnout (Mini-Z 33.3 vs 35.9, p=.257), but improved perceptions of having sufficient documentation time (2.1 vs 3.6, p=.005). Implementation facilitators: individualized training, on-site vendor support. Barriers: workflow integration challenges, editing workload, patient privacy concerns with smartphone recording. Clinicians initially used DS with straightforward patients; some expanded to all visits, others limited to specific cases. Editing workload improved over time. Most edits needed on Assessment and Plan sections. Telemedicine integration reported as feasible by those who used it.

## Hypothesis
Explicit: to evaluate DS feasibility, acceptability, appropriateness, usability, and preliminary association with clinician well-being in cancer care setting.

*📍 Only cancer care-specific study in the corpus. NCI-designated Comprehensive Cancer Center. Mixed-methods with CFIR framework for implementation analysis.*
*📍 DS rated marginally acceptable/appropriate/usable but infeasible (15.2 vs 16.0 cutoff). Improved documentation time perception but no significant burnout improvement on Mini-Z.*
*📍 Implementation insights: Individualized training and on-site/at-the-elbow vendor support critical. Patients expressed unease with smartphone recording — standardized consent processes recommended.*
*📍 Editing workload improved over time. More edits needed on Assessment and Plan sections. Telemedicine integration feasible for those who used it.*
*📍 This paper is unique in cancer care context where visits may involve complex medical histories and family caregivers. Findings suggest DS has promising utility but requires workflow integration improvements.*
*📍 Authors recommend future research with larger samples, longer follow-up, and comparison across DS platforms.*
