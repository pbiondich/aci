## Article Title
Impact of an Artificial Intelligence-Based Solution on Clinicians’ Clinical Documentation Experience: Initial Findings Using Ambient Listening Technology

## Author(s)
Galloway, J. Luke; Munroe, Dominique; Vohra-Khullar, Pamela D.; Holland, Christopher; Solis, Mary A.; Moore, Miranda A.; Dbouk, Reema H.

## DOI
[10.1007/s11606-024-08924-2](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11606-024-08924-2)

## Journal / Source
Journal of General Internal Medicine | Vol. 39 | pp. 2625–2627 | 2024. Concise Research Report. Open Access. Emory University School of Medicine / Emory Healthcare / Emory Digital.

## Ambient Product(s) Evaluated
**Abridge** (explicitly mentioned in the text). Ambient listening technology. Emory Healthcare pilot implementation.

## Clinical Setting
Emory Healthcare — large, urban integrated academic medical institution. Convenience sample of 117 clinicians at onboarding and 55 at 60-day follow-up. 31 participants completed both surveys. 58.1% male, 64.5% white, 48.4% aged 20–39, mean tenure 8.4 years, 32.3% primary care. Voluntary web-based survey.

## Evidence Level
Observational (pre/post survey study — nonrandomized pilot)

## Data Source(s)
Pre/post surveys (4 core questions + additional post-survey questions)

## Article Summary
This concise research report from Emory Healthcare evaluates the impact of Abridge ambient listening technology on clinicians' documentation experience. Pre/post surveys of 31 clinicians who completed both surveys showed significant improvements: documentation process meeting requirements (41.9% → 71%, p=0.034), ease of documentation (32.3% → 48.4%, p=0.023), documentation impact on well-being (71% negative → 38.7% negative, p=0.010), and documentation impact on patient experience (35.5% negative → 6.5% negative, p=0.005). Additional post-survey findings: 35.5% highly likely to recommend the tool, 58.1% agreed it increases productivity, 29.0% intend to use it for the majority of notes. The study is short (2 pages) and uses simple pre/post comparison without validated burnout instruments or objective EHR metrics.

## Hypothesis
Explicit: to evaluate the impact of an AI-based solution on clinicians’ clinical documentation experience.

*📍 Short research letter (2 pages). Simple pre/post survey without validated instruments or objective EHR metrics. Emory Healthcare pilot of Abridge.*
*📍 Significant improvements in all 4 core survey questions. Largest effect on patient experience perception (35.5% negative → 6.5% negative).*
*📍 35.5% highly likely to recommend; 58.1% agreed it increases productivity. Only 29% intend to use for majority of notes — adoption intention is lower than perceived benefits.*
*📍 Companion to other Emory papers? The authors are from Emory Surgery, Family Medicine, Medicine, and Emory Digital. This is an early pilot evaluation.*
*📍 Limitations acknowledged: small sample sizes, questionnaire changes between pre and post surveys, potential selection bias (convenience sample of interested clinicians).*
*📍 This paper is consistent with the self-report positive findings across the corpus but lacks the rigor of studies with objective metrics or validated instruments.*
