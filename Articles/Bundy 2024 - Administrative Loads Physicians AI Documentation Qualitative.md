# Article Review

---

## Article Title
Can the Administrative Loads of Physicians be Alleviated by AI-Facilitated Clinical Documentation?

## Author(s)
Bundy, Henry; Gerhart, Jay; Baek, Sally; Connor, C.; Isler, M.; et al.

## DOI
[10.1007/s11606-024-08870-z](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11606-024-08870-z)

## Journal / Source
Journal of General Internal Medicine | Vol. 39 | pp. 2995–3000 | 2024. Open Access. Atrium Health / Wake Forest University Health Sciences. Published June 27, 2024.

## Ambient Product(s) Evaluated
**DAX Copilot (DAXC)** — Nuance/Microsoft. GPT-4 powered generative AI clinical documentation tool. EHR-integrated version generating narrative note summaries within 30 seconds of encounter completion. Pilot at Atrium Health (multi-site academic learning health system). ClinicalTrials.gov: NCT06329427. Funded by Wake Forest University Health Sciences.

## Clinical Setting
Atrium Health — multi-site academic learning health system. 116 primary care physicians recruited to pilot DAXC. Purposive sample of 12 interviewees selected for diversity (early-career, women, minority providers overrepresented due to higher burnout risk). Semi-structured interviews August–September 2023. Interviews ~30 min, conducted via Microsoft Teams. Saturation reached after 10th interview.

## Evidence Level
Qualitative (semi-structured interviews, inductive thematic analysis)

## Data Source(s)
Semi-structured interviews (n=12 physicians) | Qualitative thematic analysis (ATLAS.ti v.9)

## Article Summary
This qualitative study at Atrium Health explored 12 primary care physicians' experiences with DAX Copilot in an early pilot via semi-structured interviews. Three major themes emerged: (1) **Benefits of DAXC** — reduced documentation time, cognitive burden relief ("cognitive offloading"), more personable patient encounters, more eye contact, reduced fear of losing clinical details; (2) **Suitable encounters** — physicians disagreed on which encounter types work best; some preferred DAXC for complex/protracted visits, others for simple acute visits, but all found edge cases where DAXC fell short; (3) **Concerns** — verbosity/inappropriately long notes (six paragraphs for a common cold), misgendering patients, hallucinations (fabricating additional patients in the room, imagined events, wrong diagnoses), negation errors ("patient did NOT" transcribed as "patient did"), verbatim/chronologically disordered narrative. A key novel concern: several physicians (n=7) worried that DAXC efficiency would be used to justify seeing more patients — framing efficiency gains as potentially threatening autonomy and work-life balance. Atrium Health subsequently expanded to 2,500 DAXC licenses.

## Hypothesis
Explicit: to evaluate physicians' experiences with DAX Copilot and assess its impact on workflows, workloads, and how it could most effectively be incorporated into practice.

## Study Measures

**D&M Dimensions reference:**
- **SQ** = System Quality | **IQ** = Information Quality | **SerQ** = Service Quality
- **U** = Use / Intention to Use | **US** = User Satisfaction | **NB** = Net Benefits

| Measure | Description | D&M Dimension(s) |
|---|---|---|
| Quality of Life / Documentation Time (qualitative) | Most interviewees (n=10) reported or expected improvement in quality of life through reduced documentation time. "Out of the week now, it may be one day that I have to come home and finish notes…at most an hour and a half." | [[NB - Net Benefits]] |
| Cognitive Burden / Offloading (qualitative) | DAXC significantly reduced anxiety about forgetting clinical details. "DAX helps a lot with cognitive offloading…you're not going to lose information like you might have before." | [[US - User Satisfaction]], [[NB - Net Benefits]] |
| Patient Engagement / Eye Contact (qualitative) | Physicians reported being more present, more attentive, better eye contact. "I'm really sort of knee to knee with them…definitely much more present." | [[NB - Net Benefits]] |
| Note Verbosity / Quality Concerns (qualitative) | Notes can be overwhelmingly verbose (n=4). "This person has a common cold and it generated six paragraphs." DAXC can't reliably distinguish relevant from irrelevant content. | [[IQ - Information Quality]], [[SQ - System Quality]] |
| Hallucinations / Errors (qualitative) | AI fabricating additional people in the room, imagining events, wrong diagnoses, misgendering, negation errors ("did not" → "did"). All interviewees noted these required editing. | [[IQ - Information Quality]], [[SQ - System Quality]] |
| Encounter Suitability (qualitative) | Disagreement among physicians on best use cases. Some preferred DAXC for complex/protracted visits; others found it counterproductive for simple short visits (<10 min). "I didn't want to use [DAXC] for very simple sick visits." | [[U - Use]], [[SerQ - Service Quality]] |
| Fear of Increased Patient Volume (qualitative) | 7/12 physicians worried DAXC efficiency would be used to mandate seeing more patients. "I do worry that's what this long game is." Novel concern not prominently captured in other papers. | [[US - User Satisfaction]], [[NB - Net Benefits]] |

*Reviewed by Iris | Source: Journal of General Internal Medicine 2024 | DOI: 10.1007/s11606-024-08870-z | Template v2.0 | Full text reviewed ✅*
*📍 Rich qualitative companion to the quantitative DAX studies. Captures the texture and nuance of physician experience that surveys miss — including specific error types and how physicians navigate them in practice.*
*📍 "Fear of increased patient volume" (7/12 physicians) is a significant and under-discussed finding. The concern that efficiency gains will translate to higher patient load threatens physician acceptance and undercuts the burnout-relief narrative. Seen also in Albrecht 2025 (47% said they could see one more patient).*
*📍 Negation errors ("did not" → "did") are a clinically dangerous error type captured qualitatively here. Not measurable in PDQI-9 but represents a specific safety risk.*
*📍 Encounter suitability disagreement: Some prefer DAXC for simple visits (predictable content), others for complex (most value added). This lack of consensus about use cases has implications for implementation guidance.*
*📍 Atrium Health subsequently expanded to 2,500 licenses — this qualitative study directly informed enterprise adoption decision.*
