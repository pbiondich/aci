## Article Title
Evaluation of an Ambient Artificial Intelligence Documentation Platform for Clinicians

## Author(s)
Stults, Cheryl D.; Deng, Sien; Martinez, Meghan C.; Wilcox, Joseph; Szwerinski, Nina; Chen, Kevin H.; Driscoll, Stephanie; Washburn, Joanna; Jones, Veena G.

## DOI
[10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2025.8614](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2025.8614)

## Journal / Source
JAMA Network Open | 2025

## Ambient Product(s) Evaluated
Abridge (identified from qualitative quotes in Table 3 — e.g., "I have loved my experience with Abridge thus far"). Paper does not explicitly name the product in the methods, but quotes make identification clear. Integrated with Epic at Sutter Health.

## Clinical Setting
Sutter Health, Palo Alto, California. 100 clinicians enrolled; 57 completed both pre- and post-implementation surveys. Mixed specialties: primary care (58%), medical subspecialty (21%), surgical subspecialty (21%). Physicians (92%) and advanced practice clinicians (8%). Pre/post survey design combined with EHR Signal data.

## Evidence Level
Observational (pre/post mixed-methods: quantitative survey + EHR telemetry)

## Data Source(s)
Survey/self-report | EHR data (Epic Signal) | Mixed

## Article Summary
This quality improvement study at Sutter Health (Northern and Central California) evaluated an ambient AI documentation platform (identified as Abridge in qualitative quotes) across 100 clinicians, with 57 completing both pre- and post-implementation surveys. Ambient AI was associated with significantly decreased time in notes per appointment (mean 6.2 → 5.3 min, p<.001), with a notable sex difference: women saw a larger reduction (8.1 → 6.7 min) than men (4.7 → 4.2 min; DiD p=.001). Documentation and progress note lengths increased slightly. NASA-TLX subscores (mental demand, temporal demand, effort) all improved significantly (all p<.001). However, Mini-Z burnout scores did not change significantly (42.1% pre vs. 35.1% post, p=.12). Clinicians reporting ≤1 hr of after-hours note writing increased from 14% to 54.4% (p<.001). Qualitative themes: improved note comprehensiveness, more patient face time, reduced cognitive load, and joy of medicine restored — balanced against EHR integration friction, inability to customize note style, and specialty-specific limitations. Off-hours EHR activity did not change significantly (p=.14 overall).

## Hypothesis
Not explicitly stated. Implicit hypothesis: ambient AI documentation will improve clinician experience of documentation burden, burnout, and EHR efficiency.

| Time in Notes per Appointment (min) | Objective EHR Signal measure: mean time clinicians spent in notes per encounter. Decreased from 6.2 to 5.3 min overall (p<.001). Significant sex-based difference (DiD p=.001): women reduced more than men. | [[NB - Net Benefits]] |
