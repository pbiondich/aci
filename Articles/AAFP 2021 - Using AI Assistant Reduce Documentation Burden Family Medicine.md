## Article Title
Using an AI Assistant to Reduce Documentation Burden in Family Medicine

## Author(s)
American Academy of Family Physicians (AAFP) Innovation Lab

## DOI
None — grey literature (AAFP Innovation Lab report)

## Journal / Source
American Academy of Family Physicians (AAFP) Innovation Lab Report | 2021

## Ambient Product(s) Evaluated
**Suki AI** — AI assistant for documentation in family medicine. Labeled "AI Assistant for Documentation" in this report; Suki confirmed from external sources (Chief Healthcare Executive, Suki's own reposting of report).

## Clinical Setting
Family medicine / primary care — AAFP Innovation Lab Phase 2. 132 family physicians and primary care clinicians across the US; 3-practice initial Phase 1 pilot followed by national Phase 2. Voluntary trial period of 30 days.

## Evidence Level
Grey Literature (professional association innovation lab report; not peer-reviewed)

## Data Source(s)
Survey/self-report (n=40 qualitative survey); product telemetry / quantitative adoption data (n=132 documentation time); operational metrics (adoption/purchasing)

## Article Summary
AAFP Innovation Lab Phase 2 report evaluating Suki AI for documentation among 132 family medicine and primary care physicians over a 30-day trial. Of 132 participants, 102 completed the trial; 61 (60%) adopted Suki as paying customers post-trial. Adopters saw a 72% reduction in median documentation time per note, translating to ~3.3 hours/week of time savings. Phase 1 (10 physicians, 3 practices) found 62% reduction in in-clinic documentation time and 76% reduction in after-hours documentation time.

## Hypothesis
Not explicitly stated. Aim: assess whether AI-assisted documentation (Suki) is essential to and readily adopted by family physicians; evaluate adoption, impact, and barriers.

## Key Findings

**Phase 2 (n=102–132):**
- **60% adoption rate** — 61 of 102 trial completers purchased Suki after trial
- **72% reduction in median documentation time per note** among adopters
- **3.3 hours/week** time savings per adopting clinician
- Participants reported improved satisfaction with workload and overall practice
- Non-adopters fell into 4 categories: (1) no significant documentation burden at baseline, (2) EMR workflow already efficient, (3) EMR integration not available, (4) couldn't fully complete the trial

**Phase 1 (10 physicians, 3 practices):**
- **62%** reduction in in-clinic documentation time
- **76%** reduction in after-hours documentation time
- Described as "a breakthrough" by some clinicians

## Contextual Notes / COI

- **Industry relationship:** AAFP called Suki an "essential innovation" for family physicians. Report featured prominently on Suki AI's resources page. This is a vendor-adjacent relationship even if not formally sponsored.
- **Grey literature:** Not peer-reviewed; methodology not fully disclosed in abstract summaries
- **72% time reduction** is an extraordinary claim — among the highest in the corpus. Compare to objective telemetry findings: Ma 2025 found −12% note time; Guo 2026 found −12% note time. Self-report vs. objective measures diverge dramatically in this field.
- **60% adoption post-trial** is a meaningful metric — patients who want to continue paying is a revealed preference signal.

## D&M Analysis

- **U (Use/Adoption):** 60% paid adoption after trial is a strong adoption signal (revealed preference, not just self-report).
- **NB (Net Benefits):** 72%/62%/76% time savings are the largest self-reported findings in the corpus — but methodology is not peer-reviewed and may not be directly comparable to validated telemetry studies.
- **US (User Satisfaction):** Improved satisfaction with workload and practice; qualitative testimonials included.
- **Relevance to Suki:** This is one of only a few papers that specifically evaluates **Suki AI** (rather than DAX Copilot or Abridge). Directly relevant to the Regenstrief/Suki partnership.
