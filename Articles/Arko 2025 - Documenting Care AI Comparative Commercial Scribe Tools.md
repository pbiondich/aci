## Article Title
Documenting Care with AI: A Comparative Analysis of Commercial Scribe Tools

## Author(s)
Arko IV, Leopold; Hudelson, Carly; Kumar, Jaya; Badlani, Sameer; Stoffel, Michelle; Markowitz, Rebecca; Melton, Genevieve B.

## DOI
[10.3233/SHTI250857](https://doi.org/10.3233/SHTI250857)

## Journal / Source
Studies in Health Technology and Informatics (SHTI) | MEDINFO 2025 — Healthcare Smart × Medicine Deep | pp. 337–341. Open Access (CC BY-NC 4.0). University of Minnesota / M Health Fairview Systems, Minneapolis MN.

## Ambient Product(s) Evaluated
**Four anonymized commercially available AI scribe solutions** (Vendor A, B, C, D). Selected based on commercial availability at time of study. No product names disclosed. No specific prompting prior to encounters — tested "out of the box."

## Clinical Setting
Simulation study — no real patients. Two simulated patient scenarios: (1) new atrial fibrillation in an outpatient medical patient; (2) new headache in a neurosurgical patient. Two physician reviewers: board-certified pediatric neurosurgeon and clinical informatics fellow (L.A.); board-certified general internist with clinical documentation improvement expertise (C.H.). Same standardized audio recordings used for all 4 vendors. Checklist of expected data elements created per encounter (14–27 elements per encounter). Friedman test for between-vendor comparisons. University of Minnesota.

## Evidence Level
Observational (simulation study — comparative note quality evaluation)

## Data Source(s)
Error taxonomy (omission, addition, incorrect facts) vs. checklist of expected elements | PDQI-9 note quality (consensus scoring) | Note length ratio

## Article Summary
This small simulation study from University of Minnesota/M Health Fairview compared 4 commercial AI scribe tools on 2 outpatient scenarios (internal medicine + neurosurgical). Error taxonomy and PDQI-9 used as primary metrics. Overall error rate: **18.9%** (range 12.2–24.4% across vendors). No statistically significant differences between vendors on any measure (p=0.345 for error rate; p=0.267 for PDQI-9; p=0.112 for note length ratio) — but the study was severely underpowered with only 2 encounters. Omissions dominated at 71% of errors (consistent with Biro 2025 and Anderson 2025). Vendor A had lowest error rate (12.2%) and balanced note length. Vendor B had highest addition errors (7.3%) and lowest PDQI-9 (35.5), especially poor on succinctness (mean 2/5). Vendor C had highest omission rate (24.4%) but shortest notes and highest PDQI-9 (39.5). Vendor D was the only one with incorrect fact errors (3.7%). Authors acknowledge the study is limited by only 2 cases and plan larger follow-up with harm severity scale.

## Hypothesis
To evaluate quality and accuracy of notes generated by 4 commercial AI scribes using simulated encounters and create a framework for institutional vendor evaluation.

*📍 Same omission-dominance finding (71%) as Biro 2025 and Anderson 2025 — a consistent cross-study pattern. Three independent simulation studies converge on omissions as the primary safety concern.*
*📍 Small sample (N=2 encounters) severely underpowers all comparisons. No results are statistically significant. Treat as a directional pilot, not definitive evidence. Authors explicitly acknowledge this and plan larger follow-up.*
*📍 Vendor C paradox: highest omission rate but highest PDQI-9 score. Short, succinct notes score well on PDQI-9 even when they're missing content. This is a PDQI-9 instrument limitation — it rewards brevity even when brevity comes at the cost of completeness.*
*📍 Vendor B note bloat: Longest notes (ratio 17.7), lowest PDQI-9 (35.5), worst succinctness (mean 2/5). Consistent with the thoroughness-succinctness trade-off seen across the corpus (Foo 2025, Palm 2025, Anderson 2025).*
*📍 University of Minnesota / M Health Fairview — adds geographic and institutional diversity to the growing simulation study cluster (OHSU/MedStar from Biro+Anderson, UCLA from Lukac, Australian GP from Foo).*
