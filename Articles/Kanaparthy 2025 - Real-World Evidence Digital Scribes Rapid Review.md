## Article Summary
This Yale-led rapid review synthesized real-world evidence on ambient AI digital scribes from 2014–2024, finding only 6 studies that met rigorous inclusion criteria (real-world clinical implementation; not solely technical development or model validation). The four major themes were: (1) **self-reported documentation time decreased** with associated note length increase; (2) **physician burnout measured by validated scales was unaffected** (contrary to popular perception), but physician engagement improved; (3) **physician productivity as measured by billing metrics was unchanged**; (4) **studies fell short when benchmarked against standardized evaluation frameworks** (QUEST and SEIPS 3.0). The review is deliberately skeptical of premature adoption, drawing parallels to robotic surgery and fitness trackers as technologies widely embraced before evidence emerged. Authors conclude digital scribes "show promise" but that "currently available evidence is sparse" and that multifaceted real-world studies are needed before unequivocal recommendation. A correction statement has been issued for this paper (see above).

## Hypothesis
Explicit: to synthesize evidence on clinician efficiency, user satisfaction, quality, and practical barriers associated with digital scribes using ambient listening and generative AI in real-world clinical settings.
