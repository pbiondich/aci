## Article Title
Effect of Ambient Voice Technology, Natural Language Processing, and Artificial Intelligence on the Patient–Physician Relationship

## Author(s)
Owens, Lance M.; Wilda, J. Joshua; Grifka, Ronald G.; Westendorp, Joan; Fletcher, Jeffrey J.

## DOI
[10.1055/a-2337-4739](https://doi.org/10.1055/a-2337-4739)

## Journal / Source
Applied Clinical Informatics | Vol. 15, No. 4 | 2024 | pp. 660–667. Thieme. University of Michigan Health-West, Wyoming Michigan. Received March 15, 2024; Accepted May 31, 2024; Published August 7, 2024.

## Ambient Product(s) Evaluated
**DAX Copilot** (Nuance/Microsoft). Primary care ambulatory settings at University of Michigan Health-West.

## Clinical Setting
University of Michigan Health-West (community-based academic health system, Wyoming Michigan). Prospective observational study with two phases: (1) **Open-label phase** — 288 patients whose physicians used DAX; patients knew DAX was being used; (2) **Masked phase** — 304 patients compared DAX encounters vs. non-DAX encounters, blinded to whether DAX was used. Primary outcome: Patient-Doctor Relationship Questionnaire-9 (PDRQ-9) score. STROBE guidelines followed.

## Evidence Level
Observational (prospective, two-arm: open-label + masked comparison)

## Data Source(s)
Patient survey (PDRQ-9 — validated 9-item patient-reported outcome measuring patient-physician relationship) | Open-label patient satisfaction questions

## Article Summary
This is the only study in the corpus using a **validated patient-reported outcome instrument** (PDRQ-9) to measure the impact of ambient AI scribes on the patient-physician relationship. Two-phase design: open-label (n=288, patients knew DAX was used) and masked (n=304, patients blinded to DAX use). Key findings: In the **open-label phase**, patients strongly agreed that physicians were more focused on them, spent less time typing, and made encounters feel more personable — positive open-label signals. However, in the **masked phase**, NO difference was detected in PDRQ-9 scores between DAX and non-DAX encounters (median 45 vs. 45; p=0.31). Adjusted OR for complete satisfaction on "how well clinician listened" was 0.8 (95% CI 0.48–1.34) — not significant. Conclusion: patients strongly endorse DAX when they know it's being used (social desirability/expectation effect), but when blinded, no measurable difference in the patient-physician relationship on a validated scale. This is a critical methodological finding for the field.

## Hypothesis
Explicit: to evaluate how use of ambient voice recognition with NLP and AI (DAX) affects the patient-physician relationship.

*📍 Only study in corpus measuring patient-physician relationship with a validated instrument (PDRQ-9). This fills the "patient experience" gap flagged in Suhail 2026's ACDQI — though the finding is a null result.*
*📍 Critical methodological insight: Open-label patients loved DAX; masked patients showed no difference. This suggests the positive patient perception seen in other studies is partly (or wholly) an expectation/social desirability artifact — patients who KNOW their physician is using an AI scribe may project positive qualities onto the encounter, but blinded patients don't see a measurable difference.*
*📍 Companion paper: Owens et al. 2024 in Family Practice (DOI 10.1093/fampra/cmad092, Paper 32 or nearby in corpus) covers provider burnout at the same institution — making this a paired patient + provider perspective study from the same site.*
*📍 The null result on PDRQ-9 is NOT the same as "patients don't like it" — it means the patient-physician relationship as measured by this instrument is unchanged, not worse. Important distinction.*
*📍 University of Michigan Health-West is a community (not academic) primary care setting — adds diversity to the predominantly academic health system focus of the corpus.*
