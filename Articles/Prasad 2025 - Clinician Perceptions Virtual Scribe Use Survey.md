## Article Title
Clinician Perceptions of Virtual Scribe Use: A Survey Study

## Author(s)
Prasad, Kriti; Frits, Michelle; Iannaccone, Christine; Melnick, Edward R.; Zhang, Jianyi; Mugal, Aqsa; Lipsitz, Stuart; Healey, Michael; Holland, Christopher; Snyder, Richard; Sinsky, Christine; Ting, David; Bates, David W.; Rotenstein, Lisa

## DOI
[10.1007/s11606-025-09771-5](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11606-025-09771-5)

## Journal / Source
Journal of General Internal Medicine | Published online August 5, 2025. Springer/Society of General Internal Medicine.

## Ambient Product(s) Evaluated
**Virtual scribes — mix of synchronous and asynchronous human/human-assisted remote scribing services** at Mass General Brigham (Brigham and Women's Hospital), Boston MA. Services used include:
- **Synchronous:** Tele-Scribe (real-time remote human scribe via desktop/laptop/mobile)
- **Asynchronous:** DAX-Nuance, Scribble, Spek (visit recorded in real time, sent to trained human scribe for post-visit documentation)

⚠️ This paper covers **virtual human scribes**, not autonomous ambient AI. DAX-Nuance here is human-assisted, not the AI version. Included in corpus as a pre-AI/hybrid comparator.

## Clinical Setting
Mass General Brigham (Brigham and Women's Hospital, Massachusetts General Hospital), Boston MA. Large academic health system. Virtual scribe program running since 2017. Survey conducted November 2022–January 2023. 450 eligible participants; 20 excluded (never used or <1 week); 430 eligible → 120 responded → 7 NPs excluded → **113 physicians analyzed** (26.3% response rate). Ambulatory physicians only. 68.1% female; 77.9% primary care; 60.2% used ≥2 years.

## Evidence Level
Observational (cross-sectional mixed-methods — quantitative survey + deductive thematic qualitative analysis)

## Data Source(s)
Survey (14-item REDCap survey) | Qualitative (free-text survey comments, deductive thematic analysis)

## Article Summary
Cross-sectional mixed-methods survey of 113 ambulatory physicians at Mass General Brigham who used virtual scribe services from 2017–2023. Overall results were mixed: 47.8% were satisfied, 66.4% reported improved wellbeing, 61.1% reported enhanced patient relationships. The most frequently cited benefits were better focus on patients during visits (50%) and reduced documentation time (53%); the most common drawback was a steep learning curve. Multivariable analyses revealed strong moderators: non-primary care physicians were substantially more likely to benefit on all three outcomes (wellbeing aOR 34.60 [5.26, 227.64]; satisfaction aOR 10.12 [2.06, 49.62]; patient relationship aOR 6.34 [1.24, 32.34]). Males were more likely than females to report improved satisfaction and patient relationships. Paradoxically, shorter scribe tenure (<2 years) was associated with dramatically reduced odds of satisfaction and patient relationship benefit — suggesting benefits accrue with adaptation time. Qualitative themes highlighted service reliability concerns (scribe turnover, training burden, variable quality), documentation problems (poor quality, paradoxical documentation time increases, errors), and workflow impacts (delayed encounter closure alongside positive impacts like more time for patients). Authors conclude specialty and gender are key moderators and call for further investigation into primary care vs. non-primary care differential.

## Hypothesis
Explicit: to characterize the relationship between virtual scribe use and physician wellbeing and identify physician characteristics associated with benefitting from virtual scribe support.

*📍 Notable: Mass General Brigham / Harvard — highest-profile academic health system in the corpus. Authors include David Bates (a foundational figure in health informatics) and Christine Sinsky (AMA, major burnout researcher).*
*📍 Virtual scribes here are human (or human-assisted), not autonomous AI. DAX-Nuance in this context = asynchronous human scribe service using Nuance technology, not the AI DAX Copilot product from other papers. Important distinction.*
*📍 Primary care paradox: Primary care physicians (78% of sample) benefited far less than subspecialists — despite having higher documentation burden. Authors attribute this to complexity of primary care notes being harder for scribes to capture, and the "hidden knowledge" expectation in primary care notes. This finding may directly predict AI ambient scribe differential benefit by specialty.*
*📍 Tenure effect: <2 years of scribe use associated with dramatically lower satisfaction and patient relationship benefit. Benefits are earned through adaptation, not immediate. Relevant for implementation timelines.*
